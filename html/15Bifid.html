Cipher #15: Bifid Cipher
Keys for the Bifid cipher consist of a 25 letter 'key square' and a period. There is no 'j' in the key-square, it is merged with the letter 'i'.

When enciphering a plaintext, each letter is replaced by the numbers on the left hand side and top of the keysquare. These are then written on top of one another as shown in step 1 (below). E.g. 'd' is in row 3, column 3 of the key square so 3 is written in the top row and 3 is written in the second row. This is done for all plaintext letters. Step 2: The numbers are then grouped into blocks of a certain size (this is called the period, and forms part of the key). In this example the period is 5. The groups are then read off left to right (this is the fractionating step that makes bifid slightly more difficult to crack than a simple substitution cipher). Step 3 shows the new sequence of numbers after reading the groups left to right, first the top row of the group followed by the bottom row. The entire string is then re-enciphered using the original keysquare (shown in step 4) e.g. 'row 3, col 2' is 'f' in the original keysquare. (Practical Cryptography)
